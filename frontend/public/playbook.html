00 — Orientation

What kind of activity is this, really?

The first thing to untangle is that your notes blend two related but different games. Knowing which is which will make everything else click.

Investing

Buying a piece of a company and holding it for years, betting the business grows. Slow, lower-stress, time does the work.

Trading (swing trading)

Buying with a plan to sell in days to months based on price behavior. Faster, more decisions, more risk. This is what most of your notes describe.

Your document mixes both — it talks about holding "core" positions for big multi-year moves and about "trading shares" you trim quickly. That's a deliberate style: keep a long-term stake while actively managing a smaller chunk around it. We'll keep flagging which is which.

01 — Foundations

The absolute basics

Your notes never define these because they assume you know them. Here's the bedrock, fast.

Stock / ShareA stock is partial ownership of a company. One unit is a share. If a company is split into 100 shares and you own 1, you own 1% of it. Share prices move all day as people buy and sell.
PositionThe total amount of a stock you currently hold. "A 10% position" means 10% of your portfolio's money is in that one stock.
LongYou own the stock and profit if it goes up. (The opposite, "short," profits when a stock falls — your notes don't use it, so ignore it for now.)
Bull / Bear marketA bull market is broadly rising; a bear market is broadly falling. The strategies in your notes are designed for bull markets — they explicitly say so.

Reading a candlestick — the single most important chart skill

A price chart is made of candles. Each candle summarizes one time period (one day, on a daily chart). It tells you four prices at a glance: where price opened, where it closed, and the high and low it reached in between.

On a green (up) candle the close is above the open. On a red (down) candle the close is below the open. The thin "wick" lines show the extreme high and low touched during the period.

Price actionJust a fancy term for "how the price has been moving" — the pattern of candles over time. "Tracking price action" means watching the chart.

Volume — the fuel gauge

Volume is how many shares changed hands in a period. It's drawn as bars under the price. It matters enormously in your notes because it reveals conviction: a price move on high volume means lots of money agrees with the move; the same move on low volume is weak and easily reversed.

InstitutionsBig professional money — funds, pensions, banks. They trade in huge size, so when they buy or sell, volume spikes. A central idea in your notes: follow the footprints of institutions, because their buying is what drives stocks up for months.
Why this matters for everything ahead

Almost every signal in your notes — a "Power Earnings Gap," an "accumulation" base, a "distribution" top — is really just price + volume telling a story about whether big money is buying or selling. Hold onto that.

02 — Foundations

Moving averages: the trader's rulebook

Your notes mention "9 EMA," "21 EMA," "50 SMA," "200-day," "10-week," and "30-week" constantly. These are all moving averages, and once you understand them, half the document decodes itself.

Moving average (MA)A line that plots the average price over the last N periods, recalculated each day. A 50-day MA today is the average closing price of the last 50 days. It smooths out the noise so you can see the underlying trend as a single line.

SMA vs EMA — the only difference that matters

SMA — Simple

Every day in the window counts equally. Smoother, slower to react. Used for the "big picture" lines (50, 200).

EMA — Exponential

Recent days are weighted more heavily, so it reacts faster to new moves. Used for the "close-in" lines (9, 21) that hug price.

The standard ladder of moving averages

Think of these as a ladder from "fast and twitchy" (top) to "slow and structural" (bottom). The further down, the more important the line and the bigger the deal if price breaks it.

LineSpeedWhat it represents in your notes
9 EMAFastestThe "trail" for strong leaders. Where you add to powerful names on a small pullback. A clean close below it can be a trim signal.
21 EMAFastA slightly deeper pullback zone. A second place to add during mild market dips.
50 SMA / 10-weekMediumThe "is the trend still healthy?" line. (50 days ≈ 10 weeks — same idea, different chart.) Strong leaders "live above" it.
200 SMA / 30-weekSlowestThe primary long-term trend line. Above it = healthy. Living below it = the big trend may be broken.

In a healthy uptrend the lines "stack" in order: price on top, then the fast MA, then the slow MAs underneath. Faster lines hug price; slower lines drift below as a safety net.

"Respecting" a moving averageWhen a stock dips down to a moving average and bounces off it instead of crashing through, it is "respecting" that line — a sign the trend is healthy and buyers are stepping in there.
"Extended"When price has run far above a moving average, it's "extended" — stretched like a rubber band, statistically likely to pull back or pause. Your notes use this to decide when to stop buying and when to trim.
The mental model to keep

Moving averages aren't magic. They're reference points that lots of traders watch, which makes them partly self-fulfilling. Your notes use them as a rulebook: where to add, when a stock is too stretched, and when a trend has broken.

05 — The Workflow

Key vocabulary you'll keep meeting

A focused glossary of the terms the strategy sections rely on. Skim now, refer back later.

Business-quality terms

EPSEarnings Per Share = company profit ÷ number of shares. The single most-watched profitability number. Rising EPS = the company is making more money per share.
RevenueTotal sales — the top line, before any costs. "Revenue growth" means the company is selling more.
YoYYear-over-Year — comparing a period to the same period a year earlier (this quarter vs. the same quarter last year). Strips out seasonal noise. Your notes want EPS and revenue growing +20% YoY.

Chart structure terms

Consolidation / BaseA period where a stock stops trending and trades sideways in a range — "resting." Your notes want a long base of 6–18+ months before buying, because a big, patient base often launches a big move. Think of it as a coiled spring.
BreakoutWhen price finally pushes above the top of a base on strong volume — often the start of the next leg up.
PullbackA temporary dip within an uptrend. Healthy. It's where your notes look to add (at the 9 EMA, 21 EMA, etc.) rather than panic.
Accumulation vs. DistributionAccumulation = institutions quietly buying (rising price on rising volume). Distribution = institutions quietly selling (heavy down-volume). Spotting distribution is the big sell warning in your notes.

Volatility terms — ADR & ATR

These two measure how much a stock typically moves. They don't predict direction — they set expectations and size your risk.

ADR (Average Daily Range)How much a stock moves in a typical day, as a percentage (usually over the last 14 days). Your notes target 3%–10% ADR: below 3% is too sluggish for swing trades (mega-caps), above 10% is wild and hard to manage (small caps). 3–10% is the tradable sweet spot.
ATR (Average True Range)The same idea but in dollars — the average daily move in price (e.g. "$1.80 a day"). Used to judge when a stock is over-stretched: if price is ~3× ATR above its 50-day average, it's statistically extended and your notes start trimming.

VWAP — the "fair price" line

VWAPVolume-Weighted Average Price — the average price paid, weighted by how much volume traded at each price. It shows the "fair value" most participants actually paid. Big institutions use it to judge if they're buying above or below the crowd's average cost.
Anchored VWAPA VWAP that starts ("anchors") from a date you pick — e.g. the stock's IPO day or an earnings day. Your notes anchor from the IPO to see the average price every holder since day one has paid. Staying above it = holders are collectively in profit = bullish.
IPOInitial Public Offering — the day a company first sells shares to the public and starts trading on an exchange.
06 — The Strategy

The two mentors: O'Neil & Weinstein

Your notes lean on two famous trader-authors. Their frameworks answer the two hardest questions: "how late is this move?" (O'Neil) and "is the big trend still on?" (Weinstein). They're complementary, not competing.

William O'Neil — bases & climax tops

O'Neil observed that big winning stocks rise in steps: they break out, run, then pause to form a new base, then break out again. He numbered these.

1

Base 1 & Base 2 — the healthy stages

Early in the advance. Fewer people have noticed, moves are cleaner, risk/reward is best. Your notes consider these the safest places to buy.

3

Base 3 & Base 4 — the late, crowded stages

By now the move is obvious, everyone's talking about it, and it's vulnerable to a sudden top. Your notes say "I believe we are at Base 3" for semiconductors — i.e. late but maybe not done.

Climax topThe end of a big run, when a stock goes vertical (a near-straight-up spike) on huge volume as the last buyers pile in — then collapses. O'Neil's late-stage warning sign. Your notes watch for this to avoid "round-tripping" (giving back) a big winner.

O'Neil's famous line, quoted in your notes: "What looks cheap often goes lower. What looks expensive often goes higher." Translation: strong, "expensive" leaders keep winning; cheap, beaten-down stocks often keep falling. Don't bargain-hunt; follow strength.

Stan Weinstein — the four stages

Weinstein simplified every stock's life into four stages, judged against the 30-week moving average (≈ the 200-day line on a weekly chart).

Stage 1: sideways base. Stage 2: the uptrend — price above a rising 30-week MA (where you want to own it). Stage 3: rounding over / topping. Stage 4: downtrend — avoid.

The rule your notes draw from Weinstein: as long as price is above a rising 30-week MA, the Stage 2 uptrend is intact — so don't get scared out of a great stock just because it's "extended." But once the 30-week flattens and price starts living below it, Stage 2 is over and the bigger trend is in question.

How they fit together

O'Neil tells you how late the move is (which base) so you don't buy a climax top. Weinstein keeps you in the trend (Stage 2) so you don't sell a winner too early. Used together: stay with strength, but stay alert for the late-stage blow-off.

07 — The Strategy

The Power Earnings Gap (PEG)

This is the headline setup in your notes — also called an Episodic Pivot (EP). It's the moment a company reports great earnings and the stock gaps up, signaling a possible "character change" where institutions pile in.

EarningsEvery quarter (≈ every 3 months) public companies report their financial results. It's the biggest scheduled event for a stock — prices often move violently right after. "Earnings season" is the few weeks when most companies report.
Gap (gap up / gap down)When a stock opens at a very different price than it closed the day before — leaving a visible "gap" on the chart. Big earnings surprises cause gaps. A gap up = good news; gap down = bad news.

The four conditions for a valid PEG

1. Strong earnings

The company beats expectations — a real fundamental catalyst, not hype.

2. The gap holds

Price gaps up and stays up — it doesn't immediately "fill" (fall back to close the gap).

3. Volume explodes

Far above average volume — proof that big money, not just retail, is buying.

4. Accumulation is visible

The price/volume behavior shows institutions stepping in, not distributing.

How your notes trade it

  • The low of the PEG candle (the lowest price on the gap day) defines your risk line — if price falls below it, the setup has failed and you exit.
  • As long as the gap holds above that low, the trend is considered intact and you stay in.
  • Targets are large (+45%, +53% in the examples) because a real character change can run for months.
Risk line / StopA predefined price where you admit you're wrong and sell, to cap your loss. Defining it before buying is the core of risk management — it turns a scary "how much could I lose?" into a known, small number.

The counter-intuitive part

Beginners see a stock gap up 15% and think "I missed it, it's too expensive now." Your notes argue the opposite — echoing O'Neil: a gap up on great earnings and huge volume is institutions starting to build positions, which can take weeks. The gap that scares retail is often the entry the pros want.

The flip side: the 3-day rule for gap-downs

The mirror-image mistake is "buying the dip" right after a stock gaps down on bad earnings — grabbing a "discount." Your notes warn against it with the 3-day rule:

The 3-day rule

After a major earnings move (especially a gap down), wait at least three trading days before acting. Big funds need days to digest the news and finish selling. Early dip-buyers are often "catching a falling knife" while institutions are still heading for the exit. Let price and volume settle and tell the story first.

Catching a falling knifeBuying a stock that's dropping fast, hoping it's near the bottom — and getting hurt as it keeps falling. The vivid warning against premature dip-buying.

Gate 1 — The chart

Deploying capital in tranches

TrancheA portion of your intended position bought separately, rather than all at once. Buying in tranches ("scaling in") lowers the risk of buying everything at a bad moment.

QQQ / SPYTwo ETFs (baskets of stocks) used as proxies for "the market." SPY tracks the S&P 500 (500 big U.S. companies); QQQ tracks the Nasdaq-100 (tech-heavy). Watching them tells you the market's overall mood.

Averaging up — the unintuitive winner's habit

Averaging upBuying more of a stock as it rises (and proves you right), raising your average cost. The opposite of "averaging down" (buying more as it falls). Your notes — citing O'Neil — say be comfortable adding to winners, not throwing money at losers.
The philosophy here

"I care more about trend + accumulation than perfect entries." The point: don't agonize over the perfect buy price on a true leader — many never give the perfect retrace. Participate in strength, but never buy a stock that's already wildly extended.

09 — The Strategy

Selling & managing — the hardest part

Your notes call selling "one of the hardest questions in trading." The guiding principle: don't sell just because a stock is up a lot — sell when the character of the move changes.

The sell framework (for the trend)

SignalAction
Stock gets extended from the 10-week (50-day) lineStop adding (but don't sell yet)
It's late-stage (O'Neil Base 3–4)Start watching for a climax top
Goes vertical after a long runTrim into strength (sell a portion while it's still rising)
Breaks 10-day or 21-day on heavy volume after a parabolic moveReduce the position
Violates the 10-week line on heavy volumeRespect the sell signal — the trend is breaking
30-week MA flattens & price lives below itStage 2 is over — question the whole trend
Trim into strengthSelling part of your position while the price is still rising (not waiting for it to fall). You lock in profit calmly instead of panic-selling on the way down. "Sell when others are greedy."
Parabolic / VerticalWhen a stock's rise steepens into a near-straight-up curve. Exciting, but usually unsustainable — a classic late-stage exhaustion sign.

Two concrete swing-trade sell methods

For shorter-term trading shares (not long-term core holdings), your notes use ATR to time exits.

A

Sell into strength using ATR extension

When price stretches to about 3× ATR above the 50-day SMA, the stock is statistically over-extended — risk/reward has flipped against you even if the trend is intact. That's when you trim. (Some indicators mark this with "green dots" on the chart.)

B

Trim & trail

At an ATR extension, sell a chunk (¼ to ⅓) to lock gains. Trail the rest using the 9-day MA — only sell the remainder after a clean close below the 9-day line. You bank some profit, stay in if the trend continues, and never try to nail the exact top.

Trailing a stopLetting your exit price "follow" the stock up — here, using the rising 9-day MA as a moving floor. As the stock climbs, your sell-trigger climbs with it, so you give back as little as possible if it reverses.
Core vs. trading shares — the key distinction

Your notes keep a "core" position while Stage 2 holds, and only trim "trading shares" when a move gets obvious, vertical, and crowded. The goal is to avoid both mistakes: selling a great winner too early and round-tripping it by ignoring a blow-off top.

12 — Putting It Together

A reality & risk check

Your notes are honest that "reality is different from books." Here's the same honesty aimed at you as a beginner, because this matters more than any setup.

The hard truths

1. Most active traders lose money. Study after study finds the large majority of retail day/swing traders are net losers over time. The skilled minority spent years and real losses getting there.

2. This is a high-risk style. Concentrated positions, +100–200% targets, and tolerating −10–20% drawdowns means real money can vanish fast if you misjudge a "thesis" or skip a stop.

A sane way to start
  • Paper trade first. Use TradingView's fake-money mode to run this whole process for months with zero financial risk until you can spot a PEG, a base, and an ATR extension on your own.
  • Learn risk before reward. The most important habit here is the risk line / stop — decide your exit before you enter, every time.
  • Read the source authors. O'Neil's How to Make Money in Stocks and Weinstein's Secrets for Profiting in Bull and Bear Markets are the primary sources your notes draw on.
  • Only ever risk money you can afford to lose entirely, and consider speaking with a licensed financial advisor about your overall plan. I'm an AI assistant, not a financial advisor.

You now have the vocabulary to re-read your original notes and actually understand every line. That's the real win — comprehension first, capital much, much later.
